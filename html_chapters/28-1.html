Establishing a New Boundary

Establishing a boundary is a procedure that can be performed in a few minutes. The difficulty lies in the purification of the boundary site. When the Community has authorized a boundary endowed with the threefold perfection, even if all its boundary markers are lost and there are no bhikkhus who know the boundary, it remains as a valid boundary for performing all Monastic Procedures until the disappearance of the Dispensation, provided it has not been revoked by the Community through a formal act. Even if the soil is removed and the boundary site is turned into a reservoir, that ground remains the boundary. For a boundary once authorized to become a non-boundary again, it must be revoked by the Community through a formal act. After revocation, even if markers such as stone pillars remain, that ground is a non-boundary.

Over two thousand years have passed since the Buddha's Dispensation was established on this island. During that long period, many monasteries have arisen in various places and fallen into ruin without leaving even a trace. There is no doubt that boundaries were established there for performing Monastic Procedures. Their markers are not visible today. Even without markers, those grounds remain valid boundaries for performing Monastic Procedures today. It cannot be said that such unknown ancient boundaries do not exist in any given place in this country. When a piece of land is selected for establishing a boundary, it may sometimes be within a larger ancient boundary. Sometimes a small ancient boundary may be entirely encompassed within it. Sometimes a portion of an ancient boundary may be encompassed. Sometimes the land may overlap with an ancient boundary.

If the ground chosen for the authorization of a boundary is entirely within an ancient boundary, it is not a new boundary but the ancient boundary itself, as one boundary cannot be authorized within another. If another bhikkhu is present within the area belonging to the ancient boundary at the time a procedure is performed within that authorized boundary, the procedure does not achieve validity due to being an incomplete group act. If no other bhikkhu is present within the ancient boundary site at the time a Monastic Procedure is performed, that procedure achieves validity.

As it is declared in the Vinaya: “Na bhikkhave! sīmāva sīmā ajjhottharitabbā. Yo ajjhottharey‍ya āpatti dukkaṭassa” (Bhikkhus, one boundary should not overlap another boundary. Whoever should overlap one, there is an offense of wrong-doing), a new boundary should not be authorized by encompassing an entire ancient boundary or a part of an ancient boundary. If done so, according to the Buddha's command: “Yesaṃ bhikkhave! sīmā pacchā sammatā, tesaṃ taṃ kammaṃ adhammikaṃ kuppaṃ aṭṭhānārahaṃ” (Bhikkhus, for those whose boundary is authorized later, that act of theirs is un-Dhamma, unstable, and fit to be set aside), that boundary does not become a valid boundary.

As it is declared: “Na bhikkhave! sīmāya sīmā sambhinditabbo, yo sambhindey‍ya āpatti dukkaṭassa” (Bhikkhus, a boundary should not be made to merge with another boundary. Whoever should merge them, there is an offense of wrong-doing), a new boundary should not be authorized by mixing it with or making it touch an ancient boundary site. If so authorized, it is also a non-boundary.

Since newly established boundaries may become invalid due to overlapping with ancient boundary sites, Vinaya experts first purify the ground when establishing a new boundary. The purification of the ground must be done by revoking the boundary through a formal act. Revoking a boundary that has existing markers and known limits is not a difficult task. When a group of no fewer than four bhikkhus enters that boundary site and recites the formal act for revocation, the revocation of the boundary occurs. In establishing a new boundary these days, what must be done is the revocation of unknown boundaries that have no markers. This is not an easy task. Therefore, it takes much time to certainly purify a piece of ground for establishing a new boundary.

There are boundaries as large as three leagues in length and breadth. There are also small boundaries. Revoking a great boundary that encompasses many villages and has unknown limits is extremely difficult. This is because while bhikkhus are gathered in one place to revoke the boundary, other bhikkhus may be present elsewhere within that great boundary. If a bhikkhu is present anywhere within that boundary without having come within arm's reach during the revocation, that act of revocation becomes an incomplete group act and the revocation of the boundary does not occur. Therefore, if ancient great boundaries are encompassed by the places where boundaries are currently being established, all those boundaries become non-boundaries. The only consoling factor in this is that great boundaries are not common. Most boundaries found in the country are less than a hundred cubits in length and breadth.

Apart from the Great Boundary of Anuradhapura, it has not been heard that a great boundary was established anywhere else in this country. Bhikkhus in the past mostly established small boundaries, just as they do now. Therefore, there should be small boundary sites in various places in the country. When bhikkhus within a range of twenty or thirty fathoms gather and perform the revocation, the revocation of those boundaries can occur. In this land, there are more places that are not encompassed by boundaries than those that are. Therefore, it is only rarely that a ground for establishing a new boundary overlaps with an ancient boundary site. Thus, if you perform the act of revocation without leaving a large area vacant of bhikkhus and purify the ground, there is no reason to doubt the boundaries being established now.
