Confession of Common Offences

“Na bhikkhave! sabhāgāpatti desetabbā. Yo desey‍ya āpatti dukkaṭassa. Na bhikkhave! sabhāgāpatti paṭiggahetabbā. Yo paṭiggaṇhey‍ya āpatti dukkaṭassa.”

According to these Buddha-provisions, an offence of wrong-doing is incurred by confessing or accepting a common offence. A “common offence” refers to an offence that both the confessor and the recipient have incurred. If a bhikkhu who has fallen into a certain offence, such as eating at the wrong time, confesses that same offence in the presence of a bhikkhu who has also fallen into that same offence, it is a confession of a common offence. If a bhikkhu who has fallen into the offence of eating at the wrong time confesses that offence along with other offences in the presence of a bhikkhu who has also fallen into the offence of eating at the wrong time, saying: “Ahaṃ bhante! sambahulā āpattiyo āpajjiṃ. Tā tumha mūle paṭidesemi”, he becomes pure from all the confessed offences.

It is not that he does not become pure from those offences because of the rule that a common offence should not be confessed. Rather, what occurs is that he becomes pure from the previous offences but incurs a new offence of wrong-doing. If the bhikkhu who accepted those offences subsequently confesses his own offence of eating at the wrong time, the offence incurred by accepting a common offence, and other offences to that same first bhikkhu, saying: “Sambahulā āpattiyo āpajjiṃ tā tumhamūle paṭidesemi”, the first bhikkhu—having already been purified of the offence of eating at the wrong time—does not fall into the fault of confessing a common offence. By confessing in this manner, that bhikkhu becomes pure from all offences remediable by confession. The first bhikkhu can then confess the offence incurred by the confession of a common offence to the second bhikkhu and become pure. Therefore, when confessing to one another, if there are common offences, the bhikkhu who confessed first must confess once more.
