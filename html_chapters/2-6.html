The Age for Ordination

“Na bhikkhave! ūnapaṇṇarasavasso dārako pabbājetabbo. Yo pabbājey‍ya āpatti dukkaṭassa.” Regarding this, the Blessed One once declared that a boy under fifteen years of age should not be given the Going Forth, and that whoever should give the Going Forth to such a one commits an offense of wrong-doing.

In later times, a very devout family who were attendants to the Venerable Ānanda Thera passed away due to the plague (ahivātaka disease). Two young children of that family survived. When they saw the bhikkhus, they would run towards them out of past familiarity. The bhikkhus would drive them away. Receiving no compassion from the bhikkhus, those children would cry. The heart of the Venerable Ānanda Thera was moved by their suffering, and thinking, “The Buddha has laid down a training rule that children under fifteen years of age should not be ordained; what can be done so that these children do not perish?” he reported the matter to the Buddha.

Then the Tathāgata asked, “Ānanda, are those children capable of scaring away crows?” When he replied, “Yes, Venerable Sir,” the Buddha addressed the bhikkhus and said: “Anujānāmi bhikkhave! ūnapaṇṇarasavassaṃ kākuḍḍepakaṃ pabbājetujaṃ”—thus allowing the Going Forth for children who have not reached fifteen years of age but are capable of scaring away crows. Although the Buddha allowed the ordination of children capable of scaring away crows to prevent those two boys from perishing in that emergency, it should be understood as the Buddha's intention that children under fifteen years of age are generally unsuitable for the Going Forth. Therefore, except for a special reason, children under fifteen years of age should not be ordained. The period between fifteen and twenty years of age should be considered the most suitable time for ordaining young men. For those who ordain late in life, it is difficult to discipline the mind and body to be suitable for the life of a renunciant. Therefore, the Buddha declared:

“Pañcahi dhammehi samannāgato dullabho vuḍḍhapabbajito. Katamehi pañcahi? Dullabho bhikkhave! buḍḍhapabbajito suvaco. Dullabho suggahitagāhī. Dullabho padakkhiṇaggāhī. Dullabho dhammakathiko. Dullabho vinayadharo.”

This means that a late-life ordinee possessed of five qualities is rare. “Bhikkhus, a late-life ordinee possessed of these five qualities is rare. Which five? Bhikkhus, a late-life ordinee who is easy to speak to is rare; a late-life ordinee who learns well from teachers is rare; a late-life ordinee who accepts the instructions of teachers respectfully is rare; a late-life ordinee who is a speaker of Dhamma is rare; a late-life ordinee who is a bearer of the Vinaya is rare.” In another Sutta, the Buddha also declared: “Dullabho ākappasampanno”—that a late-life ordinee accomplished in proper conduct and deportment is rare.

Wealth, education, or noble birth do not appear in the Buddha's teaching as factors to be considered when determining suitability or unsuitability for ordination. If one is free from the aforementioned disqualifications for the Going Forth, anyone—whether rich or poor, educated or uneducated—is suitable for ordination.

“Yebhuy‍yena hi kattiyakulato pabbajitā jātiṃ nissāya mānaṃ karonti. B‍rāhmaṇakulā pabbajitā mante nissāya mānaṃ karonti. Hīnajaccakulā pabbajitā attano vijātitāya patiṭṭhātuṃ na sakkonti. Gahapatidārakā pana kacchehi sedaṃ muñcantehi piṭṭhiyā loṇaṃ pupphamānāya bhūmiṃ kasitvā nihatamānadappā honti. Te pabbajitvā mānaṃ vā dappaṃ vā akatvā buddhavacanaṃ uggahetvā vipassanāya kammaṃ karontā sakkonti arahatte patiṭṭhātuṃ.”

This passage from the Commentary to the Cūḷahatthipadopama Sutta should also be considered in this regard.
