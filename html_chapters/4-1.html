Formal Resolve of the Bowl

Since it has been declared: “Dasāhaparamaṃ atireka patto dhāretabbo. Taṃ atikkāmayato nissaggiyaṃ pācittiyaṃ” (An extra bowl may be kept for a maximum of ten days. For one who exceeds that, there is an offence of Nissaggiya Pacittiya), a bhikkhu who keeps an extra bowl for more than ten days incurs a Nissaggiya Pacittiya offence. Therefore, a bowl must either be formally resolved or formally assigned. An extra bowl is one that has not been so treated. A bowl that should be formally resolved as a bowl is one that is neither too small nor too large, but is of a suitable size for use and has been properly finished and colored. A bowl that does not meet these criteria incurs no offence if used as a mere utensil.

A bowl received at Higher Ordination must be formally resolved within ten days. If, while one bowl is formally resolved, a new bowl needs to be formally resolved, since a bhikkhu cannot keep two bowls, the old bowl must either be given to another or formally withdrawn. When formally withdrawing a bowl, if the bowl is nearby, take it in hand or touch it and say “Imaṃ pattaṃ paccuddharāmi” (I formally withdraw this bowl) three times. If the bowl is far away, considering its location, say “Etaṃ pattaṃ paccuddharāmi” (I formally withdraw that bowl) three times. When formally resolving a bowl, if the bowl is nearby, say “Imaṃ pattaṃ adhiṭṭhāmi” (I formally resolve this bowl) three times, and if it is far away, considering its location, say “Etaṃ pattaṃ adhiṭṭhāmi” (I formally resolve that bowl) three times.

Breaking the Formal Resolve

“Accheda dāna gāhehi-vibbhamā maraṇuḍḍhaṭā

Liṅga sikkhāhi chiddesu-pattodhiṭṭhāna mujjhati.”

As indicated by the above, the formal resolve of a bowl ceases due to nine reasons: seizure by thieves, giving to others, taking by trusted persons, abandoning the robes, death, formal withdrawal, change of gender, rejection of Higher Ordination, and becoming so holed that rice grains fall out. If the formal resolve is broken due to a hole, it must be repaired and formally resolved again. If not done so, a Nissaggiya Pacittiya offence is incurred after ten days.

Formal Assignment of a Bowl

If a bhikkhu who has formally resolved one bowl receives other bowls and wishes to keep them in his possession, he must formally assign them. If he keeps them without doing so, a Nissaggiya Pacittiya offence is incurred after ten days. Formal assignment should be done as follows: Take the bowl to a competent bhikkhu, squat, and say the phrase “Imaṃ pattaṃ tuyhaṃ vikappemi” (I formally assign this bowl to you) three times, and give it to that bhikkhu. Formal assignment is not a gift or an offering, but a Monastic Procedure. Therefore, that bhikkhu must return it, saying the phrase “Mayhaṃ santakaṃ paribhuñja vā vissajjehi vā yathāpaccayaṃ karohi” (Use what belongs to me, or dispose of it, or do as you deem fit) three times. This act is called formal return (paccuddhāra).

After the formal return, keeping and using that bowl for as long as desired incurs no offence. When formally assigning multiple bowls, one should say “Ime patte tuyhaṃ vikappemi” (I formally assign these bowls to you), and during the formal return, “Mayhaṃ santake paribhūñja vā vissajjehi vā yathā paccayaṃ karohi” (Use what belongs to me, or dispose of them, or do as you deem fit). If the bowl is not nearby for formal assignment, tell the other bhikkhu where your bowl is, and considering its location, say “Etaṃ pattaṃ tuyhaṃ vikappemi” (I formally assign that bowl to you), or if there are many bowls, “Ete patte tuyhaṃ vikappemi” (I formally assign those bowls to you).

Size of the Bowl

“Tayo pattassa vaṇṇā ukkaṭṭho patto majjhimo patto omako patto. Ukkaṭṭho nāma patto aḍḍhāḷhako danaṃ gaṇhāti. Catubhāgaṃ khādanaṃ tadupiyaṃ ca vyañjanaṃ. Majjhimo nāma patto nālikodanaṃ gaṇhāti. Catubhāgaṃ khādanaṃ tadupiyaṃ ca vyañjanaṃ. Omako nāma patto patthodanaṃ gaṇhāti. Catubhāgaṃ khādanaṃ tadupiyaṃ ca vyañjanaṃ” This is a passage from the Pārājikāpāḷi that describes the size of a bowl.

This Monastic Procedures discourse describes three types of bowls: the excellent bowl, the medium bowl, and the inferior bowl. The excellent bowl is one that can hold all of the following: rice well-cooked from two nāḷis of rice (a measure used in Magadha at that time), a mung bean curry cooked to a consistency that can be taken by hand, approximately one-fourth the amount of the rice, and other curries suitable for that rice. The medium bowl is one that can hold all of the following: rice cooked from one nāḷi of rice from Magadha, a mung bean curry one-fourth the amount of that rice, and other curries sufficient for that rice. The inferior bowl is one that can hold all of the following: rice cooked from half a nāḷi of rice from Magadha, a mung bean curry one-fourth the amount of that, and other curries.

If, when two nāḷis of rice from Magadha, one-fourth of that amount of mung bean curry, and other curries are placed in a bowl, the contents remain below the lower rim of the bowl, that bowl is too large for an excellent bowl and is therefore an unsuitable bowl. A bowl in which the prescribed amount of food for an inferior bowl overflows the rim is too small and thus unsuitable for formal resolve; it is also an unsuitable bowl. In the Vinaya commentary, nine types of bowls are mentioned: excellent-excellent, excellent, excellent-inferior, medium-excellent, medium, medium-inferior, inferior-excellent, inferior, and inferior-inferior. Of these, the excellent-excellent bowl and the inferior-inferior bowl are stated to be unsuitable bowls, while the remaining seven are suitable for formal resolve.

There are various opinions regarding the Magadha nāḷi. It can generally be considered to be about the size of the *seruva* currently in use here.

It should also be noted that, apart from an iron bowl and a clay bowl, as declared: “Anujānāmi bhikkhave! Dve patte ayopattaṃ mattikāpattaṃ” (Bhikkhus, I allow two bowls: an iron bowl, a clay bowl), any other type of bowl is unsuitable for formal resolve.
