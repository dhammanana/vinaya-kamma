Matters to be Known Regarding the Fulfillment of Vatta and Mānatta.

According to the Western system, a day consists of twenty-four hours. According to the astrological system, a day consists of sixty ghaṭikā. In the Western system, the beginning and end of a day occur at midnight. In the astrological system, the beginning of the day is considered from sunrise. According to the Vinaya method, the beginning of the day is considered from dawn (aruṇodaya).

“Sūrassodayato pubbuṭṭhitaraṃsi siyāruṇo”

In the Abhidhānappadīpikā, it is stated that the first rising ray of the sun before it appears is called 'aruṇa'. 'Aruṇa' is also a name for a light reddish color. That name is given to the first rising ray of the sun because that ray possesses a light reddish hue. That light is visible in the eastern sky about half an hour before sunrise. With the appearance of that light in the sky, according to the Vinaya method, the passing of the previous day and the beginning of the present day are considered.

When fulfilling vatta and mānatta, a day is counted not by dwelling under the undertaking for the full number of hours in a day, but by being under the undertaking at the time of dawn. Regardless of which hour of the twenty-four hours of the day the vatta is undertaken, if it is maintained until dawn, it is considered as one day gained. If a bhikkhu who has undertaken the vatta in the morning relinquishes (nikkhepa) the vatta even a moment before dawn, that bhikkhu does not gain the day. Here, the counting of days is done at dawn.

During the time of dwelling having undertaken vatta and mānatta, if even a bhikkhu traveling at a distance is seen, or if the voice of a bhikkhu is heard, an announcement (ārocana) must be made to that bhikkhu. If it is not done, an interruption of the night (ratticcheda) occurs. There is also an offense of wrong-doing (dukkaṭa) due to the breach of duty (vattabheda). The meaning of 'ratticcheda' is that the day does not become a day that is counted. If one makes an effort to announce but is unable to do so, there is no offense, but ratticcheda still occurs. Therefore, it is difficult to fulfill the vatta in places where many bhikkhus come and go, or in places where bhikkhus traveling at a distance are visible. Because there are bhikkhus traveling at a distance and because they are visible, it is also difficult to fulfill the vatta during the daytime.

The vatta cannot be fulfilled in a place where there are no bhikkhus at all. For parivāsa to be fulfilled, at least one other bhikkhu must be present. For mānatta to be fulfilled, a group of no fewer than four bhikkhus must be present. Therefore, if there are no other bhikkhus in the monastery where you reside, you must go to a monastery where there are bhikkhus, or you must invite bhikkhus to the monastery where you reside. It is also difficult to fulfill the vatta in monasteries situated near main roads where vehicles constantly travel, because when bhikkhus traveling in vehicles are seen, one does not get the opportunity to announce to them. If the monastery where the bhikkhu fulfilling the vatta resides is a suitable place for it, he should fulfill the vatta there. If not, he should go to a suitable monastery and fulfill the vatta. If you see bhikkhus traveling at a distance and cannot make the announcement, ratticcheda will occur; therefore, undertake the vatta during the night and relinquish (nikkhepa) it after dawn. Do so until the required number of days is completed.
