Views from a Modern Book

In a modern book by a certain Mahathera, who has made a great effort to interpret commentarial passages in a way that would invalidate a Kathina robe cut, sewn, and dyed by donors, it is stated that there are two ways of giving: the Community itself making a robe from the cloth received from the donor and giving it to the monk who is the spreader, or giving the cloth exactly as it was received. He states:

“Evaṃ dinne pana kaṭhine sace taṃ niṭṭhitaparikammaṃ hoti iccetaṃ kusalaṃ. No ce niṭṭhitaparikammaṃ hoti sabbaheva sannipatitvā dhovanasibbanarajanāni niṭṭhāpetabbāni”

He claims this commentarial passage indicates that if the Kathina cloth assigned by the Community to the spreader monk does not have the preliminary work completed, everyone must assemble to finish the washing, sewing, and dyeing. That commentarial passage indeed shows that after the Community assigns the Kathina cloth to a monk, all monks should participate in the washing and other tasks. However, in that modern book, it is said: “In both cases—assigning and then making the robe, and making the robe and then assigning—the Community must be united. If any remain apart, there is an offense of wrong-doing.”

We do not know where that Thera discovered that an offense of wrong-doing occurs by not participating in the making of the robe *before* the assignment, as this is not mentioned in the commentary. If the intention of the commentator in the passage beginning with Evaṃ dinne pana kaṭhine was to show that everyone must unite to finish the Kathina robe, there would be no need for such a long sentence. In that case, it should simply say: “Evaṃ dinne pana kaṭhine sabbeheva sannipatitvā dhovana sibbana rajanāni niṭṭhāpetabbā.” If stated thus, it would clearly express that one must participate in the making of the Kathina robe at any stage, without many words and without confusion.

If the purpose were solely to show that one must participate in the making of the robe, inserting the phrase “Sace taṃ niṭṭhitaparikammaṃ hoti iccetaṃ kusalaṃ no ca niṭṭhitaparikammaṃ hoti” into the middle of the sentence would be useless. When that is included, it does not say that the Community must unite if the Kathina robe is being made before it is given to a monk. Would the commentators, who possessed sharp intelligence and were extremely proficient in composition, create such a confusion? When the commentator said “Sace taṃ niṭṭhitaparikammaṃ hoti,” he was referring not to a robe made by the Community, but to a robe made and offered by a donor. When the meaning is taken in this way, there is no confusion at all.

Although the modern book mentions a custom of the Community making the Kathina robe and later assigning it to the spreader monk, it is not mentioned anywhere in the Pali, the Commentaries, or the Sub-commentaries that there is a custom of performing the preliminary work before the Community gives the Kathina cloth to a monk. That is merely an opinion of that Thera. The manner in which the Community should proceed when a Kathina cloth arises is stated in the Parivara Pali as follows:

“Sace saṅghassa kaṭhinadussaṃ uppannaṃ hoti. Saṅghena kathaṃ paṭipajjitabbaṃ? At‍thārakena kathaṃ paṭipajjitabbaṃ? Anumodakena kathaṃ paṭipajjitabbaṃ? Saṅghena ñattidutiyakammena kaṭhinatthārakassa bhikkhuno dātabbaṃ. Tena kaṭhinatthārakena bhikkhunā tadaheva dhovitvā vimajjitvā vicāretvā jinditvā sibbetvā rajitvā kappaṃ katvā kaṭhinaṃ attharitabbaṃ.”

The meaning is: ‘If a Kathina cloth arises for the Community, how should the Community proceed? How should the spreader proceed? How should the one who appreciates proceed? The Community should give it to the spreader monk by a formal act consisting of a motion and a second. That spreader monk, on that very day, having washed it, pressed it, examined it, cut it, sewn it, dyed it, and marked it, should spread the Kathina.’

There, only the assignment of the cloth by the Community to the spreader monk is mentioned, not the assignment of a finished robe. It is also evident from the cited Parivara passage that the preliminary work belongs to the spreader, not the Community. In describing the qualities of a monk suitable for the spreading of the Kathina, the fact that “he knows the preliminary work” (Pubbakaraṇaṃ jānāti) is preached as the first quality because the preliminary work is a task belonging to the spreader monk. The task belonging to the Community is to assist the spreader monk in the preliminary work.

The Community making the Kathina robe before assigning it to a monk also contradicts the Parivara passage: “Kaṭhinassa pubbakaraṇaṃ ādi kiriyā majjhe atthāro pariyosānaṃ.” (The preliminary work of the Kathina is the beginning, the act is the middle, and the spreading is the end). If the preliminary work is done before assigning the Kathina, then the assignment of the robe would fall into the middle of the Kathina process. In the Parivara, the two acts of relinquishing and determination are shown as the middle of the Kathina process. Therefore, the method of the Community making a robe and giving it to the spreader—

Must be described as a method of assigning the Kathina fabricated by that Mahathera in order to confuse the meaning of the commentarial passage beginning with “Evaṃ dinne pana kaṭhine.” Even though such a custom is not mentioned in the Monastic Procedures, it cannot be said that it results in a failure of the Kathina.

The commentarial passage “Karaṇavipannaṃ nāma tadaheva chinditvā akataṃ” is also cited in that book to show that a robe made by donors is unsuitable for the Kathina. This is an excellent commentarial passage to cite for the purpose of deceiving those who do not know the Monastic Procedures. Anyone who does not know the facts stated in the Kathina-kkhandhaka regarding the Kathina, and who does not know about the three failures of the Kathina, can be deceived by citing that passage. The meaning of the commentarial passage “Karaṇavipannaṃ nāma tadaheva chinditvā akataṃ” can only be correctly understood by looking at all three failures of the Kathina together.

Three failures of the spreading of the Kathina are mentioned in the Parivara Pali: “failure of object, failure of time, and failure in making.” Among these, “failure of object” means the Kathina cloth being an unallowable cloth. If the Kathina cloth is an unallowable cloth unsuitable for a robe, even if the preliminary work and other tasks are performed and the Kathina is spread with it, it does not achieve success.

As stated in the commentary, “Kālavipannaṃ nāma ajja dāyakehi dinnaṃ sve saṅgho kaṭhinatthārakassa deti”—if the Community does not give the Kathina cloth received today to the spreader monk today, but gives it tomorrow—the Kathina robe becomes a failure of time. A spreading of the Kathina performed with a robe that has failed in time also does not achieve success.

As stated in the commentary, “Karaṇavipannaṃ nāma tadaheva chinditvā akataṃ”—a robe where the preliminary work was not done on the same day the Community gave the cloth to the spreader monk, but was completed on a later day, is called a failure in making. A spreading of the Kathina performed with such a robe also does not achieve success.

All the rules and regulations regarding the Kathina do not concern cloth or robes found in shops or with donors, but rather cloth or robes offered to the Community. There is no rule in the Monastic Procedures stating that a donor must offer only cloth and not a robe for the Kathina, or that if a robe is given, it should not be accepted, or that the Kathina should not be spread with it. If a donor offers a finished robe for the Kathina, the Community should accept it and give it to the spreader monk.

Since the preliminary work such as cutting and sewing has already been done, there is no need for the spreader monk to wash out the dye and cut and sew it again, thinking, ‘I must complete the preliminary work.’ Since those tasks have already been performed on that robe, there is no failure in making if they are not done again. It is endowed with the success of making. It would be a great absurdity to destroy a robe that has been cut, sewn, and dyed, claiming it is to achieve the success of making. The Buddha does not prescribe such useless work. Therefore, it should be understood that spreading the Kathina with a robe previously made by a donor does not result in a Kathina failed in making; rather, a robe becomes failed in making only by performing the preliminary work on a day later than the day the cloth was received.
