The Meditation on the Fivefold Skin-Constituents

  1. These hairs of the head, being a part of the body: are loathsome because of their foul smell, because they originate from impurities such as bile, phlegm, blood, pus, feces, and urine, and because they arise within the heap of thirty-one impurities.
  2. These hairs of the body, being a part of the body: are loathsome because of their foul smell, because they originate from impurities such as bile, phlegm, blood, pus, feces, and urine, and because they arise within the heap of thirty-one impurities.
  3. These nails, being a part of the body: are loathsome because of their foul smell, because they originate from impurities such as bile, phlegm, blood, pus, feces, and urine, and because they arise within the heap of thirty-one impurities.
  4. These teeth, being a part of the body: are loathsome because of their foul smell, because they originate from impurities such as bile, phlegm, blood, pus, feces, and urine, and because they arise within the heap of thirty-one impurities.
  5. This skin, being a part of the body: is loathsome because of its foul smell, because it originates from impurities such as bile, phlegm, blood, pus, feces, and urine, and because it arises within the heap of thirty-one impurities.

By way of elements:

  1. These hairs of the head, being a part of the body: are a portion of the earth element (paṭhavī-dhātu), which is not a living being, not a person, not a self, and has no owner.
  2. These hairs of the body, being a part of the body: are a portion of the earth element (paṭhavī-dhātu), which is not a living being, not a person, not a self, and has no owner.
  3. These nails, being a part of the body: are a portion of the earth element (paṭhavī-dhātu), which is not a living being, not a person, not a self, and has no owner.
  4. These teeth, being a part of the body: are a portion of the earth element (paṭhavī-dhātu), which is not a living being, not a person, not a self, and has no owner.
  5. This skin, being a part of the body: is a portion of the earth element (paṭhavī-dhātu), which is not a living being, not a person, not a self, and has no owner.

The candidate for ordination should continue this meditation without neglect even after going forth.

After the hair is shaved, the candidate must bathe and cleanse himself thoroughly with soap to remove the scent of the laity. If the candidate is unable to do so himself, you, as the teacher, must bathe and cleanse the candidate for going forth (pabbajjāpekkha). In doing so, act out of compassion, without feeling disgust, just as a father would bathe a beloved son. Thereafter, having dressed him in clean clothes, lead the candidate to the place of ordination. Upon arrival, if the parents of the clansman (kulaputta) are present, he should first pay homage to their feet and take leave of them. Then, having paid homage to the feet of the preceptor (upajjhāya) and the assembled bhikkhus, he should take the robes in his hands, sit in the squatting position (ukkutika) before the preceptor, and with hands in añjali, request the going forth. The formula for this is as follows:

Okāsa! Ahaṃ bhante! Pabbajjaṃ yācāmi.

Dutiyampi ahaṃ bhante! Pabbajjaṃ yācāmi.

Tatiyampi ahaṃ bhante! Pabbajjaṃ yācāmi.

Sabba dukkha nissaraṇa nibbāna sacchikaraṇatthāya imaṃ kāsāvaṃ gahetvā pabbājetha maṃ bhante! Anukampaṃ upādāya.

Dutiyampi sabba dukkha nissaraṇa nibbāna sacchikaraṇatthāya imaṃ kāsāvaṃ gahetvā pabbājetha maṃ bhante! Anukampaṃ upādāya.

Tatiyampi sabba dukkha nissaraṇa nibbāna sacchikaraṇatthāya imaṃ kāsāvaṃ gahetvā pabbājetha maṃ bhante! Anukampaṃ upādāya.

Having recited this formula and offered his robes into the hands of the preceptor, he should again sit in the squatting position before the preceptor and, with hands in añjali, recite this formula:

Sabba dukkha nissaraṇa nibbāna sacchikaraṇatthāya etaṃ kāsāvaṃ datvā pabbājetha maṃ bhante anukampaṃ upādāya. Dutiyampi sabba dukkha -pe- upādāya. Tatiyampi sabba dukkha -pe- upādāya.

When the candidate has requested the robes with this formula, you, as the teacher, should place the shoulder-strap (aṃsa) around the candidate's neck and give him the robes you hold for the purpose of dressing and robing. The candidate should then reflect upon the robes received from the teacher and put them on. If the candidate is unable to dress himself, the teacher or another should assist him. One should not put on the robes as a layman without receiving them from a teacher. If the candidate is already wearing robes, they must be removed and then given back to him for the ordination. If the Refuges are undertaken without doing so, the going forth is not established. It is also permissible for another to dress him upon the preceptor's command. If the preceptor gives the robes to a layman saying, "Dress him in these robes," it is permissible for the layman to do so by the preceptor's command. After being robed, the candidate should return to the preceptor, pay homage to his feet with the fivefold prostration, sit in the squatting position with hands in añjali, and request the ten precepts of going forth along with the Triple Refuge using this formula:

Okāsa! Ahaṃ bhante tisaraṇena saddhiṃ pabbajjā dasasīlaṃ dhammaṃ yācāmi: anuggahaṃ katvā sīlaṃ detha me bhante. Dutiyampi ahaṃ bhante -pe- me bhante. Tatiyampi ahaṃ bhante -pe- me bhante anukampaṃ upādāya.

When this has been said, the preceptor should say, “Yamahaṃ vadāmi taṃ vadehi” and have him recite the Namaskāra, and then:

Buddhaṃ saraṇaṃ gacchāmi.

Dhammaṃ saraṇaṃ gacchāmi.

Saṅghaṃ saraṇaṃ gacchāmi.

.

Dutiyampi Buddhaṃ saraṇaṃ gacchāmi.

Dutiyampi Dhammaṃ saraṇaṃ gacchāmi.

Dutiyampi Saṅghaṃ saraṇaṃ gacchāmi.

.

Tatiyampi Buddhaṃ saraṇaṃ gacchāmi.

Tatiyampi Dhammaṃ saraṇaṃ gacchāmi.

Tatiyampi Saṅghaṃ saraṇaṃ gacchāmi.

The Refuges should be undertaken either by joining the words with the niggahita ending (e.g., “Buddhaṃ saraṇaṃ gacchāmi”) or by separating the words with the 'm' ending (e.g., “Buddham saraṇam gacchāmi”). It is better to have them undertaken in both ways. Many teachers do so. Even if the training rules such as “Pāṇātipātā veramaṇī sikkhāpadaṃ samādiyāmi” are not recited, the status of a novice (sāmaṇera) is attained through the undertaking of the Refuges alone. The recitation of the training rules is not the primary factor here.

Since the intention (cetanā) is the primary factor in a layman's taking of refuge, the taking of refuge occurs regardless of the language or manner in which the training rules are recited. However, for a novice, the taking of refuge occurs only when the teacher recites the words in the Pali language without error in pronunciation or articulation (ṭhāna-karaṇa-sampatti), and the pupil likewise recites them without error. If even one of the two—teacher or pupil—is unable to pronounce correctly, the taking of refuge does not occur. Elderly persons without teeth cannot pronounce them perfectly. Therefore, when an elderly thera without teeth ordains a pupil, he should have another person administer the Refuges.
