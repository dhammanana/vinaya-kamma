Failures of Monastic Procedures.

A failure of a procedure (kamma-vipatti) refers to a defect in the formal act. A procedure performed incorrectly is not a valid act. Therefore, when performing Monastic Procedures, they must be conducted with absolute purity, ensuring no defect occurs at any point.

“Imāni cattāri kammāni katihākārehi vipajjanti? Pañcahākārehi vipajjanti, vatthuno vā ñattito vā anusāvanato vā sīmato vā parisato vā”.

As stated in the Parivāra, the four types of procedures mentioned above fail in five ways: regarding the basis (vatthu), the Motion (ñatti), the proclamation (anusāvana), the boundary (sīmā), and the assembly (parisa). These constitute unrighteous acts (adhamma-kamma).

“Sammukhā karaṇīyaṃ kammaṃ asammukhā karoti vatthuvipannaṃ adhammakammaṃ” etc. As stated in the Parivāra, if a procedure that should be performed in the presence of the individual is performed in their absence; if a procedure that requires questioning is performed without questioning; if a procedure that requires a confession is performed without a confession; if a verdict of mindfulness (sati-vinaya) is given to one who should receive a verdict of past insanity (amūḷha-vinaya); if amūḷha-vinaya is given to one who should receive an act of further misconduct (tassapāpiyyasikā-kamma); if tassapāpiyyasikā-kamma is performed on one who should receive an act of censure (tajjaniya-kamma); if tajjaniya-kamma is performed on one who should receive an act of subordination (niyassa-kamma); if niyassa-kamma is performed on one who should receive an act of banishment (pabbājanīya-kamma); if pabbājanīya-kamma is performed on one who should receive an act of reconciliation (paṭisāraṇiya-kamma); if paṭisāraṇiya-kamma is performed on one who should receive an act of suspension (ukkhepaniya-kamma); if ukkhepaniya-kamma is performed on one who should receive probation (parivāsa); if parivāsa is given to one who should receive a sending back to the beginning (mūlāya paṭikassana); if mūlāya paṭikassana is performed on one who should receive penance (mānatta); if mānatta is given to one who should receive rehabilitation (abbhāna); if abbhāna is performed for one who should receive Higher Ordination (upasampadā); if upasampadā is performed for one who should receive another act; if Uposatha is performed on a non-Uposatha day; if Pavāraṇā is performed on a non-Pavāraṇā day; if a eunuch (paṇḍaka) is given Higher Ordination; or if one who has gone over to another sect (titthiyapakkantaka), an animal, a matricide, a patricide, a killer of an Arahant, a violator of a nun, a schismatic, one who has shed the blood of a Buddha, a hermaphrodite (ubhatobyañjanaka), or one under twenty years of age is given Higher Ordination—these are unrighteous acts due to failure of the basis (vatthu-vipanna).

In times of danger, the following eight acts are suitable to be performed even in the absence of the individual: Higher Ordination of a nun via a messenger, the overturning of the bowl (pattanikkujjana), the authorization for one insane (ummattaka-sammuti), the authorization for a trainee (sekha-sammuti), the Brahma-punishment (brahmadanda), the act of public proclamation (pakāsanīya-kamma), and the act of non-salutation (avandiya-kamma). All other procedures must be performed in the presence of the individual concerned. If performed in their absence, those acts are unrighteous acts due to failure of the basis. They are acts contrary to the Monastic Procedures. In procedures requiring questioning, the questioning of the individual is the basis itself; if performed without questioning, the act becomes unrighteous due to failure of the basis. In performing acts like Higher Ordination for unsuitable persons, the unsuitability of those persons itself constitutes the failure of the basis.

“Pañcahākārehi ñattiyo kammāni vipajjanti. Vatthuṃ na parāmasati, saṅghaṃ na parāmasati, puggalā na parāmasati, ñattiṃ na parāmasati pacchā vā ñattiṃ ṭhapeti. Imehi pañcahākārehi ñattiyo kammāni vipajjanti”. As stated in the Parivāra, procedures fail regarding the Motion due to these five factors: not mentioning the basis, not mentioning the Community, not mentioning the individual, not mentioning the Motion, or placing the Motion last.

Therein, "not mentioning the basis" means reciting the Motion without including the name of the person for whom the act (such as Higher Ordination) is being performed. Where one should say, “Suṇātu me bhante saṅgho ayaṃ nāgo āyasmato tissassa upasampadāpekkho”, if the name of the candidate for Higher Ordination is omitted and the formula is recited as “Suṇātu me bhante saṅgho āyasmato tissassa upasampadāpekkho”, this is a failure to mention the basis.

Where one should say, “Suṇātu me bhante saṅgho ayaṃ nāgo āyasmato tissassa upasampadāpekkho”, if the word “saṅgho” is omitted and one says, “Suṇātu me bhante ayaṃ nāgo āyasmato tissassa upasampadāpekkho”, this is a failure to mention the Community.

"Not mentioning the individual" means omitting the name of the preceptor of the candidate for Higher Ordination. Where one should say, “Suṇātu me bhante saṅgho ayaṃ nāgo āyasmato tissassa upasampadāpekkho”, if one says, “Suṇātu me bhante saṅgho ayaṃ nāgo upasampadāpekkho”, this is a failure to mention the individual.

"Not mentioning the Motion" means omitting the Motion entirely and only reciting the proclamation. "Placing the Motion last" means reciting the proclamation first and then saying, “Esā ñatti”, followed by “Khamati saṅghassa tasmā tuṇhī evametaṃ dhārayāmi”.

“Pañcahākārehi anusāvaṇato kammāti vipajjanti. Vatthuṃ na parāmasati, saṅghaṃ na parāmasati, puggalaṃ na parāmasati. Sāvaṇaṃ hāpeti, akāle sāveti imehi pañcahākārehi anusāvaṇato kammāni vipajjanti”. As stated in the Parivāra, procedures fail regarding the proclamation due to these five factors: not mentioning the basis, not mentioning the Community, not mentioning the individual, omitting the proclamation, or proclaiming at the wrong time.

Here too, the failure to mention the basis and so forth should be understood as explained regarding the Motion. "Omitting the proclamation" means reciting only the Motion and leaving out the proclamation. It also includes two factors: mispronouncing letters and words. Not only the proclamation but also the Motion must be recited without error. By the bhikkhus reciting the procedural formula—

“Sithilaṃ danitañca dīgharassaṃ

Garukaṃ lahukañca niggahitaṃ,

Sambandhaṃ vavatthitaṃ vimuttaṃ

Dasadhā vyañjanabuddhiyāppabhedo.”

It must be performed considering these ten factors mentioned in the verse. Sithila refers to unaspirated characters. Dhanita refers to aspirated characters. Niggahīta refers to the nasal sound (bindu) which should be pronounced by sending air through the nose with the mouth neither fully open nor fully closed. Vimutta refers to characters that should be pronounced by opening the mouth and releasing the breath without tightening the vocal organs. These four factors must be specially considered when reciting the procedural formula. The Vinaya Commentary states that pronouncing an unaspirated sound as aspirated, an aspirated as unaspirated, a nasal as released, or a released as nasal causes the failure of the act (kamma-kopa), while errors in the other six factors do not cause the act to fail. Nevertheless, when performing Monastic Procedures, the formula should be recited such that purity is maintained regarding all ten factors. "Proclaiming at the wrong time" means reciting the proclamation before the Motion.

Procedures performed within the eleven types of defective boundaries fail due to boundary failure (sīmā-vipatti). The eleven defective boundaries have been explained previously in the section on the Monastic Procedures for defining boundaries.

“Dvādasahi ākārehi parisato kammāni vipajjanti” etc. The Parivāra declares that a procedure fails due to the assembly in twelve ways. Failure due to the assembly occurs through three factors: the required number of suitable bhikkhus not being present within the boundary, not taking the consent (chanda) of those bhikkhus from whom it should be taken, or the procedure being blocked by some present bhikkhus expressing dissent. These three factors, when applied to the four categories of procedures (those requiring a group of four, etc.), result in twelve ways an act fails due to the assembly.
