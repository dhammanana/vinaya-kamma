The Monastic Procedure for the Binding of a Boundary

If the binding of a boundary is not performed in accordance with the Disciplinary Code, not a single monastic procedure performed therein will be valid. Therefore, the binding of a boundary is a monastic procedure that must be performed with extreme caution, great care, and utmost purity. To perform it purely, one must know the eleven defects of a boundary and the threefold perfection of a boundary.

“Ati khuddakā, ati mahati, khaṇḍanimittā, chāyānimittā, animittā, bahi sime ṭhitasammatā, nadiyā sammatā, samudde sammatā, jātassare sammatā, simāya simaṃ sambhindantena sammatā, simāya simaṃ ajjhottharantena sammatāti imehī ekādasahī ākāretī simato kammāni vipajjanti”

As it has been declared, the eleven defective boundaries are: a boundary that is too small, a boundary that is too large, a boundary with broken marks, a boundary authorized by shadow marks, a boundary authorized without proclaiming marks, a boundary authorized while standing outside the boundary, a boundary authorized in a river, a boundary authorized in the sea, a boundary authorized in a natural lake, a boundary authorized by mixing it with an ancient boundary, and a boundary authorized by overlapping an ancient boundary. Monastic procedures performed within such boundaries do not achieve validity.

(1) A boundary that is too small is one that does not have enough space for twenty-one bhikkhus to sit and perform a monastic procedure.

(2) A boundary that is too large is one that exceeds three leagues (yojanas) in length or breadth even by a small amount.

(3) A boundary with broken marks (khaṇḍanimitta) is one authorized without connecting mark to mark during the proclamation. Marks are the boundary indicators. When authorizing a boundary, the Vinaya expert must start from one point and ask, “Puratthimāya disāya kiṃ nimittaṃ?” and so on. Upon receiving the answer, he must proclaim the marks in order, such as “Eso pāsāṇo nimittaṃ”. In doing so, after proclaiming the final mark, the first proclaimed mark must be proclaimed again so that it connects with the last. If one stops at the proclamation of the final mark without doing this, it becomes a boundary with broken marks. It is a non-boundary. Furthermore, a boundary authorized by taking something unsuitable as a mark is also called a khaṇḍanimitta boundary.

(4) A shadow-mark boundary is one authorized by taking the shadow of a tree, mountain, or any other object as a mark.

(5) A markless boundary is one authorized without proclaiming the marks, even if marks have been placed.

(6) A boundary authorized while standing outside is one authorized by proclaiming the marks while standing outside of those marks.

(7,8,9) As it has been declared, “Sabbā bhikkhave nadi asīmā, sabbo samuddo asimo, sabbo jātassaro asimo”, boundaries authorized in a river, the sea, or a natural lake are not valid boundaries.

(10) A boundary authorized by including a small portion of a previously authorized boundary is also a non-boundary.

(11) A boundary authorized by including an entire previously authorized boundary, or a portion of it large enough for four bhikkhus to sit, is a boundary authorized by overlapping an ancient boundary. This too is a non-boundary.

The threefold perfection consists of: perfection of marks, perfection of the assembly, and perfection of the proclamation.

Marks are the boundary indicators of the ground being authorized. As it is stated: “Paṭhamaṃ nimittā kittetabbā, pabbatanimittaṃ, pāsāṇanimittaṃ, vananimittaṃ rukkhanimittaṃ magganimittaṃ vammikanimittaṃ nadinimittaṃ udakanimittaṃ”, there are eight objects that may be taken as marks for a boundary. They are: a mountain, a stone, a forest, a tree, a road, an anthill, a river, and water.

Mountain Mark (Pabbatanimitta)

There are three types of mountains: earth mountains, stone mountains, and mixed earth-and-stone mountains. If an elevation is smaller than the size of an elephant, it is not reckoned as a mountain. Sand dunes are also not reckoned as mountains. Therefore, hills not as high as an elephant and sand dunes are not taken as boundary marks. Where there are four mountains on four sides, a boundary authorization can be made by proclaiming them as marks. When there are three mountains, the binding of a boundary can be done with three marks. The binding of a boundary cannot be done with only one or two marks. If a portion of a mountain is to be included within the boundary ground, do not proclaim the mountain itself as the mark; instead, take a tree, stone, or anthill situated upon it as the boundary mark. The mark must always remain outside the boundary ground. Two connected mountains should be taken as a single mark, not as two marks. If a curved mountain lies on two or three sides of the boundary ground, it should be taken as a mark for only one direction. Even if the mountain exists, something else must be taken as a mark for the other sides. If the entire mountain is included within the boundary ground, trees or stones situated outside it must be taken as marks.

Stone Mark (Pāsāṇanimitta)

A stone belonging to any type is suitable as a boundary mark. Iron-slag is also reckoned as stone; therefore, it is suitable as a boundary mark. A stone as large as an elephant or larger should be proclaimed as a mountain mark, not as a stone mark. What should be proclaimed as a stone mark are stones smaller than an elephant. A heap of small pebbles or a brick should not be taken as a stone mark. The Vinaya Commentary states: “Battiṃsa pala guṇapiṇḍa parimāṇo vaṭṭati na tato khuddakataro”, meaning a stone smaller than a lump of sugar weighing thirty-two palas should not be taken as a boundary mark.

According to the medical usage in this country, twenty madati seeds make one kalañda. Twelve kalañdas make one pala. By that measure, thirty-two palas is approximately five and three-quarter pounds. It is said that the measure used in the Magadha country is twice the common worldly measure, and that the thirty-two palas should be taken by the Magadhan measure. Since there are various systems of measurement, the pala mentioned in the Commentary is uncertain. However, since such large lumps of sugar are unlikely, it cannot be said that stone pillars about two or three feet high, taken as boundary marks, are insufficient in size.

In Burma, Vinaya-observant Elders do not bind boundaries by setting up stone pillars as marks. They dig holes, fill them with water, and bind the boundary using water marks. Later, they set up stone pillars in those places to identify the locations. A rock plateau, no matter how large, can be taken as a stone mark. If one wishes to include a portion of a large rock plateau within the boundary ground, do not take the rock plateau as a boundary mark; instead, take something else upon it as the boundary mark. If the same rock plateau lies on two or three sides of the boundary ground, it should be taken as a mark for only one side. The same rock plateau should not be proclaimed as two or three marks. Something else must be taken as marks for the other sides.

Forest Mark (Vananimitta)

A forest made of trees without heartwood, such as palmyra, coconut, or kitul, is unsuitable to be taken as a boundary mark. What should be taken as a boundary mark is a forest made only of trees with heartwood, or one mixed with trees having heartwood. To be considered a forest, there must be at least four or five trees. Two or three trees should not be taken as a forest mark. No matter how large the forest taken as a mark is, there is no defect in its greatness. If a boundary is being bound in the middle of a forest, the forest should not be taken as a mark. Even when including a portion of a forest in the binding of a boundary, the forest should not be considered a mark. In a place where there is no forest within the boundary ground but a forest exists around that ground, the surrounding forest can be proclaimed as a mark for one direction.

Tree Mark (Rukkhanimitta)

A tree taken as a tree mark must also belong to a species that has heartwood. Trees where the heartwood is on the outside, such as bamboo, rattan, coconut, or areca, are not suitable to be taken as boundary marks. If it belongs to a species with heartwood, a living tree at least eight inches high and as thick as a stylus is suitable as a boundary mark. Even if large, a dead tree is unsuitable. A tree rooted in the ground is suitable, but a tree in a pot is unsuitable as a boundary mark. If one wishes to make a tree planted in a pot a boundary mark, it must be planted in the ground. A tree just planted, whose survival is uncertain, should also not be taken as a boundary mark. There are banyan trees that spread over a large area by rooting from their branches. Such a tree should be proclaimed as a mark for only one place, not for two or three places, because even though it is rooted in many places, it is still a single tree.

Road Mark (Magganimitta)

Short paths leading to gardens, fields, tanks, or ponds are unsuitable as boundary marks. What should be taken as boundary marks are long roads that pass through at least two or three villages. If a foot-path starts from a carriage-road and then falls back into that same carriage-road and ends, that path should also not be taken as a boundary mark. Even if it is a long road, if it has been abandoned by travelers, it is unsuitable as a boundary mark. What is suitable for proclamation as a road mark is a road used by travelers that passes through at least two or three villages. If two roads starting separately have joined at some point, only one of them, not both, should be made boundary marks. Boundary marks must be distinct from one another.

Anthill Mark (Vammikanimitta)

An anthill built on that very day, eight inches high and as thick as a cow's horn, is suitable to be taken as a boundary mark. An anthill smaller than that is not suitable to be taken as a boundary mark. Even if it is as large as a hill, an anthill is suitable as a boundary mark. If there is an anthill connected around the boundary ground, it must be proclaimed as a mark for only one direction.

River Mark (Nadinimitta)

During the rainy season, when it rains once every ten or fifteen days, if the flow of water stops as soon as the rain stops, it does not reach the status of a river. If, as stated, during the time of rain in the rainy season, the water flows without stopping, and if there is enough water to wet the inner robe of a bhikkhunī dressed with the three circles covered when she enters the river at a crossing or non-crossing point to cross over, then that river is a suitable river for a boundary mark and other monastic procedures. Even if the flow of water has stopped during the dry season, the river does not become a non-river. At that time too, it is suitable to be taken as a river mark.

If the flow of water in a small river has been stopped by people building dams, it is thereafter not suitable to be taken as a river mark. If the water flows over the obstruction, it is suitable as a river mark. What is suitable for proclamation as a river mark is flowing water. If standing water is proclaimed as a boundary mark, it must be done as a water mark. A river flowing around the boundary ground should be taken as a mark for only one direction, not for two or three directions. If there are rivers around the boundary ground that have no connection to each other, they can be taken as marks for those respective directions. A canal cut to take water from a great river, even if water flows constantly, is not suitable as a boundary mark. If that watercourse dug by people later becomes large due to the flow of much river water, and becomes a river where boats and crocodiles dwell, then it is suitable to be taken as a river mark. No specific length or breadth is given for a river suitable to be taken as a mark.

Water Mark (Udakanimitta)

Standing water collected anywhere on the ground that does not flow is suitable as a water mark. There is no fixed measure for it. Water collected in a hole dug by pigs or dogs, or in a hole dug by children for play, is suitable to be taken as a water mark. Even water filled into a hole just dug is suitable to be taken as a boundary mark if it lasts until the end of the recitation of the formal act. When a boundary is bound using such a small amount of water as a mark, stone pillars should be placed in the locations where the water used as marks was situated, so that the boundary ground can be identified in the future. Or some other permanent sign should be established.

The authorization of a boundary is for the purpose of performing Community acts. Since it is not a matter concerning the ownership of land, there is no fault for the bhikkhus in placing stone pillars in those places. In the authorization of a boundary, there is no question regarding land ownership. A boundary can be bound on land belonging to anyone. The ground does not become the property of the Sangha because of the binding of a boundary.

For the monastic procedure of binding a boundary to be possible, an assembly of no fewer than four bhikkhus must gather. The binding of a boundary is done within a village boundary. Therefore, when binding a boundary, all bhikkhus staying within that village who have not entered a bound boundary must be brought to that place. The consent (chanda) of those bhikkhus who cannot be brought must be obtained. By doing so, the binding of the boundary is endowed with the perfection of the assembly. If there is even a single bhikkhu within the village where the boundary is being bound who has not given consent and has not come to the place, the binding of the boundary will not be valid due to the defect of the assembly.

In some villages, there may be defective boundaries bound improperly by those who are not experts in the Vinaya. Since such boundaries are non-boundaries, if there is a bhikkhu who has not given consent within such a non-boundary in the village where the boundary is being bound, the new boundary also becomes a non-boundary. Since the status of previously existing boundaries may not be known, the most certain method when binding a boundary in any village is to bring all the bhikkhus living in that village to the site, and to remove any doubtful bhikkhus from the village before binding the boundary. By binding the boundary while reciting the formal motion and proclamation prescribed by the Buddha for the binding of a boundary without error, the monastic procedure of binding a boundary is endowed with the perfection of the proclamation.
