Those Unfit for Going-forth

There are two classes of persons unfit for Going-forth: those who are excluded from Going-forth such that even if the ceremony is performed, the status is not attained; and those for whom Going-forth is prohibited because it is unseemly, though the status would be attained if the ceremony were performed. A teacher incurs an offense of wrongdoing (dukkaṭa) by granting Going-forth to those who are unfit.

There are eleven persons excluded from Going-forth:

  1. Paṇḍako bhikkhave! anupasampanno na upasampādetabbo, upasampanno nāsetabbo.
  2. Ubhatobyañjanako bhikkhave! anupasampanno na upasampādetabbo, upasampanno nāsetabbo.
  3. Theyyasaṃvāsako bhikkhave! anupasampanno na upasampādetabbo, upasampanno nāsetabbo.
  4. Titthiyapakkantako bhikkhave! anupasampanno na upasampādetabbo, upasampanno nāsetabbo.
  5. Tiracchānagato bhikkhave! anupasampanno na upasampādetabbo, upasampanno nāsetabbo.
  6. Mātughātako bhikkhave! anupasampanno na upasampādetabbo, upasampanno nāsetabbo.
  7. Pitughātako bhikkhave! anupasampanno na upasampādetabbo, upasampanno nāsetabbo.
  8. Arahantaghātako bhikkhave! anupasampanno na upasampādetabbo, upasampanno nāsetabbo.
  9. Lohituppādako bhikkhave! anupasampanno na upasampādetabbo, upasampanno nāsetabbo.
  10. Saṅghabhedako bhikkhave! anupasampanno na upasampādetabbo, upasampanno nāsetabbo.
  11. Bhikkhunīdūsako bhikkhave! anupasampanno na upasampādetabbo, upasampanno nāsetabbo.

The eleven persons mentioned in these eleven disciplinary rules—the paṇḍaka, ubhatobyañjanaka, theyyasaṃvāsaka, titthiyapakkantaka, tiracchānagata, mātughātaka, pitughātaka, arahantaghātaka, lohituppādaka, saṅghabhedaka, and bhikkhunīdūsaka—are excluded from both Going-forth and Higher Ordination. Even if they are given the Going-forth, the status is not established. Even if they are given the Higher Ordination, the status is not established.

Paṇḍaka refers to persons with a deficiency in gender characteristics. There are five types of paṇḍaka: the āsittapaṇḍaka, the usūyapaṇḍaka, the opakkamikapaṇḍaka, the pakkhapaṇḍaka, and the napuṃsakapaṇḍaka. One who calms his sexual fever by taking another's genitals in his mouth and sucking the impurity is called an āsittapaṇḍaka. One who calms his sexual fever by watching others engage in sexual intercourse is called an usūyapaṇḍaka. One who has been castrated, as is done to dogs or cattle, is called an opakkamikapaṇḍaka. One who, through the power of unwholesome kamma, becomes a paṇḍaka during the waning half of the month is called a pakkhapaṇḍaka. One who is born without either male or female gender characteristics is called a napuṃsakapaṇḍaka. Among these five types of paṇḍaka, Going-forth is not prohibited for the āsitta and usūya paṇḍaka. The remaining three are unfit for Going-forth. Even for the pakkhapaṇḍaka, Going-forth is prohibited only during the period he remains a paṇḍaka. During that time, he becomes crazed with sexual fever. The sexual fever of the āsitta and usūya paṇḍaka can be calmed through wise reflection (yoniso manasikāra) and other means. The sexual fever of the pakkhapaṇḍaka cannot be so calmed. That is why he is unfit for Going-forth during that period.

Ubhatobyañjanaka refers to a person in whom both male and female characteristics are present. There are two types: the female ubhatobyañjanaka and the male ubhatobyañjanaka. Among them, the female ubhatobyañjanaka is capable of both conceiving and causing other women to conceive. The male ubhatobyañjanaka can cause women to conceive but cannot conceive himself. Both are capable of performing both male and female roles during sexual gratification.

There are three types of theyyasaṃvāsaka: the liṅgatthenaka, the saṃvāsatthenaka, and the ubhayatthenaka. One who, without receiving Going-forth from a teacher, dons the robes himself, does not declare his seniority in rains, does not accept salutation according to seniority, does not obstruct other bhikkhus by taking a seat, and does not participate in formal acts of the Community (vinaya kamma), but merely steals the outward appearance of a recluse, is called a liṅgatthenaka. One who has received only the novice Going-forth from bhikkhus but goes to another region and falsely declares, "I have this many rains," accepts salutations from bhikkhus according to seniority, obstructs bhikkhus by taking a seat, and presents himself at formal acts of the Community, is called a saṃvāsatthenaka because he steals only the communion. One who dons the robes himself, goes to a monastery, declares a number of rains, accepts salutations from bhikkhus, obstructs bhikkhus by taking a seat, and participates in formal acts of the Community, is called an ubhayatthenaka. A detailed explanation of the theyyasaṃvāsaka is found in the Samantapāsādikā, which should be consulted.

Titthiyapakkantaka refers to one who, having been ordained as a bhikkhu in the Buddha's Dispensation, later takes up the going-forth of another sect and adopts their views.

Tiracchānagata here refers not only to animals but to all non-human beings such as nāga, supaṇṇa, deva, and brahmā. Going-forth is a privilege reserved for human beings alone.

Mātughātaka refers to one who has committed the act of matricide, which is one of the weightier kammas with immediate result (ānantariya kamma). This kamma occurs when a child of the human race intentionally takes the life of a mother who is also of the human race.

The same applies to the pitughātaka (patricide). Even if one does not know that the person being killed is one's father, the ānantariya kamma is committed by the act of killing. Details should be consulted in the Samantapāsādikā. An explanation of these kammas is also found in the 'Handbook of the Buddhist'.

Arahantaghātaka should also be understood in terms of human Arahants. If a human intentionally takes the life of an Arahant of the human race, whether ordained or not, it is an ānantariya kamma. He cannot receive Going-forth. If one kills an Arahant who is not human, it is only a grave sin, not an ānantariya kamma. If an animal takes the life of a human Arahant, the ānantariya kamma does not occur; it is only a grave sin.

Lohituppādaka refers to one who, like Devadatta, with a malicious mind, causes even a single drop of blood to be shed from the body of a living Buddha. For him, Going-forth and Higher Ordination are prohibited. For one who draws blood from the Buddha's body with a mind of loving-kindness in order to cure an illness, there is merit and no sin.

Saṅghabhedaka refers to one who, like Devadatta, causes a schism in the Community by means of any of the four types of formal acts. This is a kamma that can only be committed by one who has received Higher Ordination, not by one who has not.

Bhikkhunīdūsaka refers to one who has violated a regular bhikkhuni in any of the three passages. For him, Going-forth and Higher Ordination are prohibited. For one who has merely compromised the virtue of a bhikkhuni through bodily contact, Going-forth and Higher Ordination are not prohibited.
